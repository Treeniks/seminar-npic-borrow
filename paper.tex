\section{The Theory of Logic and Types} \label{sec:theory}

Type systems and mathematical logic have unintuitively deep ties. The notion of type inference in modern languages is based mostly on Hindley-Milner type systems, its name originating from the fact that Hindley (a logician) and Milner (a computer scientist) found the same system independently of one another. Hence, to talk about the theory of types, we must first have a peek at the theory of logic.

\subsection{Intuitionistic Logic} \label{sec:intuitionistic:logic}

Logic provides formal languages and inference rules for precise reasoning. When logic is formalized, proofs become mathematical objects that can be analyzed. Intuitionistic logic is one such formal system. It's primary differentiator from classical logic is the lack of the law of excluded middle. In classical logic, for any proposition \(A\), the formula \(A \lor \lnot A\) is a tautology. In intuitionistic logic, this is not generally provable.~\cite{mints-intro-intuitionistic-logic}

With this limitation, intuitionistic logic is a logic of constructive proofs. Classical logic allows proofs of a proposition that don't actually construct a specific witness, proofs by contradiciton being the typical culprit. In intuitionistic logic, one must explicitly provide the corresponding constructive evidence.

Since we are mostly concerned with general concepts, we will only view a reduced version of the logic used by Philip Wadler (TODO ref here). Its grammar can be seen in \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:logic:grammar}. For the sake of simplicity, we exclude \emph{disjunctive} connectives (\(\lor\)).

To do logical reasoning, we the need rules for such reasoning, the likes of which are seen in \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:logic:nd}.

We have the \emph{axiom} Id, the three \emph{structural} rules Exchange, Contraction and Weakening, and \emph{logical} rules for \emph{implications} (\(\rightarrow\)-I and \(\rightarrow\)-E) and \emph{conjunctives} (\(\land\)-I and \(\land\)-E). Note also that many other equivalent formulations exist, including ones that don't require explicit structural rules, but the one presented here makes the transition to linear types easier.

A rule like these describes a step of logical reasoning. The core idea is that, when the things above the line hold, the things below the line hold as well. Hence, there are two ways to interpret a rule:
\begin{description}
  \item[Top to bottom]
    If I know the thing at the top holds, I can deduce that the thing at the bottom holds
  \item[Bottom to top]
    If I want to show the thing on the bottom, I first need to show the thing at the top
\end{description}
The second interpretation becomes particularly relevant when writing proofs. One would starts with the statement one wants to proof, and by applying the rules of natural deduction from bottom to top, it becomes clear what is needed to proof first. The first interpretation on the other hand is how we normally convey proofs to others. One starts with the axioms or known facts, and keeps deriving new facts until the goal is reached.

Actual proofs are usually written as proof trees, where each branch is an instantiation of a rule and leaves are axioms. For example, \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:logic:proof} shows the tree that proofs \(A \rightarrow B, A \vdash A \land B\).

Of particular interest for us are the Contraction and Weakening rules. Contraction being the ability to duplicate truths as much as we like, while Weakening is the ability to forget truths.

\subsection{Intuitionistic Types} \label{sec:intuitionistic:types}

We will now move away from logic and into the theory of computation and types. To talk about types we first need a programming language, for which we will use a small, limited and typed lambda calculus, the grammar of which can be seen in \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:types:grammar}. We write \(t : \alpha\) to denote that term \(t\) has type \(\alpha\).

  \begin{subfigure}{\textwidth}

      | \(\lambda x\ldotp s\) : Function Definition | \(s\ t\) : Function Application
      | \((u, v)\) : Tuple Definition | let \((x, y) = t\) in \(w\) : Tuple Destructuring
    \end{bnf}

    \caption{Intuitionistic Terms}
  \end{subfigure}

  \begin{subfigure}{\textwidth}
    \centering
    \begin{bnf}
      \(\tau, \pi, \omega\) : Type ::= \(T\) : Base Type
      | \(\tau \rightarrow \pi\) : Functions
      | \(\pi \times \omega\) : Tuple
    \end{bnf}

    \caption{Intuitionistic Types}
  \end{subfigure}

  \caption{Grammar of Untyped Lambda Calculus} \label{fig:intuitionistic:types:grammar}

Similarly to when we defined natural deduction rules to define the notion of logical reasoning, here we will define typing judgements which outline what types certain terms have. We write \(\Gamma \vdash t : \tau\) to say that, under the typing context \(\Gamma\), the term \(t\) has type \(\tau\) (and hence \(t\) is well typed). Typing contexts are a list of assignments from variables to types \(x_0 : \tau_0, x_1 : \tau_1 \dots x_n : \tau_n\). The judgement rules can be seen in \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:types:judgements}.

    \RightLabel{Exchange}
    \UnaryInfC{\(\Gamma, \Delta \vdash t: \tau\)}
  \end{prooftree}

    \DisplayProof\qquad

    \UnaryInfC{\(\Gamma, x : \tau \vdash s : \pi\)}
  \end{prooftree}

  \begin{prooftree}
    \AxiomC{\(\Gamma, x : \tau \vdash t : \pi\)}
    \RightLabel{\(\rightarrow\)-I}

    \DisplayProof\qquad

    \AxiomC{\(\Delta \vdash t : \tau\)}
    \RightLabel{\(\rightarrow\)-E}

  \end{prooftree}

  \begin{prooftree}
    \AxiomC{\(\Gamma \vdash u : \tau\)}

    \DisplayProof\qquad

\subsubsection{Curry-Howard correspondence}

Astute readers, or those who have seen the Curry-Howard correspondence before, will have noticed that both the judgements in \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:types:judgements} as well as the example proof presented in \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:types:proof} are effectively identical to those in \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:logic:nd} and \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:logic:proof} respectively.

This is the discovery made by (TODO authors Curry-Howard): propositions are types, and terms are proofs. The conjunction (\(A \land B\)) for propositions are pairs (\(\tau \times \pi\)) in the world of terms and types, implications (\(A \rightarrow B\)) are functions (\(\tau \rightarrow \pi\)) and terms uniquely represent a proof tree (modulo certain commutative properties TODO philip wadler ref).

This last point in particular seems magical at first and the correspondence isn't quite as obvious as the others. However, the example we used so far actually shows this connection quite well: The term \((x, f\ x)\) gives significant insight into what a proof of \[A \rightarrow B, A \vdash A \land B\] needs to do. The fact that the variable \(x\) appears twice in the term means there must be a use of the \emph{Contraction} rule, the fact the term is a tuple definition means there must be a use of the \(\land\)\nobreakdash-I rule, and the existence of a function application \(f\ x\) means the proof contains a use of the \(\rightarrow\)\nobreakdash-E rule.

We mentioned how intuitionistic logic is a system of constructive proofs in \autoref{sec:intuitionistic:logic}. With the Curry-Howard correspondence, this idea becomes even more concrete: In order to prove a proposition, we must be able to construct a term of the corresponding type. Such a term is a program that, when executed, constructs a value of this type.

\subsection{Linear Logic} \label{sec:linear:logic}

% https://www-users.cse.umn.edu/~arnold/502.s97/complex.pdf
% page 13
%
% alts:
% https://mccuan.math.gatech.edu/courses/6321/lars-ahlfors-complex-analysis-third-edition-mcgraw-hill-science_engineering_math-1979.pdf
% page 138
% also page 78 of https://noter.math.ku.dk/koman-12.pdf
Linear logic is the idea that not all truths are free. With intuitionistic logic, we could duplicate and forget truths as much as we wanted, and in a mathematical sense, that is what we want. If one proves the \emph{Maximum Modulus Theorem}, that theorem can be further used to proof \emph{Schwarz's Lemma} as well as the \emph{Phragmén-Lindelöf Theorem}~\cite{conway-complex}. We don't somehow lose access to the \emph{Maximum Modulus Theorem} after proving \emph{Schwarz's Lemma}. However, duplication is not free in the real world. If \(A\) means \enquote{I have 60 rupees}, \(B\) means \enquote{I can buy 30 arrows} (that cost 60 rupees total) and \(C\) means \enquote{I can buy a Deku Shield} (which costs 40 rupees), then \autoref{fig:linear:logic:rupees} shows a proof of how 60 rupees can buy both in intuitionistic logic by duplicating \(A\). And yet, 60 rupees aren't actually enough to buy both 30 arrows \emph{and} a Deku Shield.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \begin{prooftree}
    \AxiomC{\(A \vdash B\)}
    \AxiomC{\(A \vdash C\)}
    \RightLabel{\(\land\)-I}
    \BinaryInfC{\(A, A \vdash B \land C\)}
    \RightLabel{Contraction}
    \UnaryInfC{\(A \vdash B \land C\)}
  \end{prooftree}

  \captionof{figure}{Proof of \(A \rightarrow B, A \vdash A \land B\)} \label{fig:linear:logic:rupees}
\end{figure}

The issue is with the Contraction and Weakening rules, both of which linear logic tries to limit. Unfortunately, this is where the details get a bit messy in order for linear logic to remain powerful enough that everything stays expressable. We will hence refrain going into these details and only give an overview.

The core idea is to split propositions into two camps: linear and intuitionistic. Intuitionistic propositions, now denoted \([A]\), work as we know and allow for Contraction and Weakening, while linear propositions, denoted \(\langle A\rangle\), allow everything but Contraction and Weakening. The notation for the logical connectives also changes, and their meaning may now differ.

\(A \rightarrow B\) becomes \(A \multimap B\) and whereas before we could freely convert \(A\) into \(B\), if \(A\) is linear the operation completely consumes our \(A\). \(A \land B\) becomes \(A \otimes B\) and while there is not a big difference in meaning, there are other formulations of conjunctives that are equivalent in intuitionistic logic, but have a profoundly different meaning in linear logic.

Note that for our example proof from \autoref{fig:intuitionistic:logic:proof}, if we make the assumption \(A\) linear, we get: \[A \multimap B, \langle A\rangle \vdash A \otimes B\] But this isn't provable anymore, as we would need to duplicate \(\langle A\rangle\).

\subsection{Linear Types} \label{sec:linear:types}

Applying the Curry-Howard correspondence to linear logic, we get a particularly useful type-theoretic concept: Non-duplicatable and non-forgettable values. Taking a look at our example proof once again: \[f : \tau \rightarrow \pi, x : \tau \vdash (x, f\ x) : \tau \times \pi\] The thing of interest is the duplication of \(x\). In a purely theoretical lambda calculus, this may be fine, but in real world programming \(x\) could be anything, including a highly limited resource. If \(x\) is a simple integer, it may be no problem to copy it like that, but if \(x\) is a large array, copying may be an unacceptable performance hit. Even worse, if \(x\) represents some resource like a file, duplication may be catastrophic.
